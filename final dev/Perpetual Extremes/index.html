How heat vulnernability has become a lethal new normal in NYC



The term "extreme weather event" typically conjures thoughts of blizzards or hurricanes. Indeed, a Google image search of "extreme
weather NYC" will show you multiple pages of what you would imagine: apocolyptic flooding, a man digging his car out from two feet
of snow, or twisted building carnage from Hurricane Sandy--rare but thorough in its destruction.



Yet New York City regularly experiences a different extreme weather event not shown in our search results. Heat waves (defined as
three or more days above 90 degree Farenheit) have started occurring with disturbing frequency and duration due to global warming
and an increasingly unforgiving urban heat island.


These are the longest heatwaves in NYC's history since 1895

Source: Adapt NY

It can be hotter inside during a heat wave


This unique study tracked indoor temperatures within a 7th floor southeast-facing apartment
(without AC) during August of 2016. People often believe they're taking refuge from extreme
temperatures by staying indoors but this is not always the case. Even when it cools down outside,
building materials trap heat inside and occupants are subjected to higher ambient temperatures.

Source: Harlem Heat Project

12.5% of NYC Seniors Lack AC at Home


Senior citizens are the most vulnerable population during an extreme heat event, especially
those without air conditioning at home. The city does provide free air conditioners to low-income
seniors, but many aren't able to assume the utility bills. And even though cooling centers are
available throughout the city, this low-mobility population is more inclined to stay at home.

Source: NYC Environment and Health Data Portal (2013)

Learning from Chicago

The 1995 Chicago heat wave is equally known for its high death toll and poor response
by city officials. Over 650 people died, and the iconic images of refrigerated mobile
morgues brought in to handle the overflow of bodies seemingly shamed city officials into
overhauling their heat response plan.

A similar heat wave in 1999 yielded a far lower death toll. This was primarly due to the city
providing free and direct transportation to cooling centers and having police officers go
door-to-door, checking on seniors and dispatching EMTs when necessary.

NYC currently does not include these practices in their heat emergency response plan.
